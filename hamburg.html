From the Bible Belt to Deutschland: Hamburg, Germany

When the walls start to close in on you. When you can’t take another trip on the Q train. When another bacon egg and cheese just won’t cut it. What do you do? You pack a fresh pair of undies, every lens you own, your favorite camera body and you jet set across the pond to a world totally unfamiliar, with the purpose of the trip being to experience something outside the realm of your personal knowledge. Visiting the ancient and humbling city of Hamburg, Germany gave me that opportunity to engage in that experience. Situated in the north on the Elbe River and established circa 800 AD the medieval-esk city continues to pay homage to its past with yearly festivals like the Port Anniversary. And although I’m sad to say true German cuisine evaded my palate, a delicacy of the time currywurst I did try and it was amazing. The citizens and the very land their ancestors settled continue to be connected while most live off the land in one-way or another. Evident by the wind turbines, livestock, and straw roofs that dot the countryside. The relationship between neighbors is a very modest and respectful one. You’d be hard pressed to find a loud mouth here and the youth make way for their elders to pass uninhibited. Boasting the 2nd largest port in Europe, the city is very affluent. Even with tragedies like the Great Fire of 1840 that all but wiped out the city and the decimation of the largest concentration of Jews during WWII, the city still seems very grounded. This eastern European destination provides you an excellent opportunity to time travel in both directions. Architecture like the modern Elbphilharmonie and the nearly 400-year-old St. Michael’s Church coexist with ease even in a world where the generations find it hard too bridge the gap between the abacus and the iPad. Hamburg will undoubtedly continue to remind us of a better time in society as long as Otto Von Bismark keeps his watchful gaze over this historic German state.
